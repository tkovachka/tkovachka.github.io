I knew I wanted to do something with code in the 7th grade. 

That year I studied abroad - in London, 
and there, for the first time in my life I wrote some code that made a little **monkey game**.
All you could do was walk the monkey left and right and make it do a back-flip, but it was enough to 
pique my interest. Then I forgot about it all throughout high school - until I enrolled in university. 
And sure, I didn't exactly turn out as a game-maker, but the little monkey stirred me in the direction of
coding, of developing, of making something come alive from just an idea - a thought. 

Then in December 2025 I graduated with a **Bachelor's degree in Software Engineering and Information Technologies.**
Right in time for **AI to takeover writing code**.

AI agents can now produce in 3 prompts what used to take me months and endless stack-overflow threads. 
At first, I felt threatened by it. I had spent 4 years developing skills that were now irrelevant. 
Wasted my money and time to become someone who was now not needed. 
So, I admit it took me some time to start "trusting" AI and what it output. I was convinced that I could do it better.

**Then I woke up**. Once I accepted that AI is the winner on *writing* the code, I could start focusing on more important things.
I started treating AI agents as _colleagues_, as developers who can help make my idea a reality - faster. If before I wanted to build
a house from scratch, now I only have to give my agents the floor plan, and let them build the house for me.
I can now focus on my **creativity, innovation, research**. Things that I am actually passionate about. 
Now, AI handles the _grunt work_ and I get to spend my time on exploring new ideas and making the decisions that
actually matter.

This newfound freedom gave me space to **dig even deeper** into data science and machine learning -
not because I wanted to become an ML engineer, but because I wanted to understand what
was **under the hood**. How these models are built, what they are at their **core**, where
they're strong and where they quietly hallucinate. That understanding slowly crystallized
into a deep interest in **LLMs** specifically: **context** engineering, **prompt** engineering, and
what happens when you wire a simple API call to a language model into an otherwise
ordinary software project.
